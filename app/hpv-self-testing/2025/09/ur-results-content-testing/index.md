Results are a critical part of the HPV self-testing journey, as they determine next steps for users. We carried out multiple rounds of testing to ensure the content is clear, accurate and supports decision-making.

We ran:

-	one unmoderated round in September
-	one moderated round in October, following content iterations

## Research aims

-	Review result content for clarity, readability and use of clinical terminology
-	Assess user understanding of next steps based on their result
-	Identify gaps in content and uncover new user needs

## Recruitment

We worked with organisations embedded in communities we want to reach to ensure a diverse participant group.

Participants were recruited via:

-	Black Health Initiative, supporting engagement with Black communities, who are less likely to attend cervical screening due to distrust, previous negative experiences and not feeling listened to in healthcare settings
-	Trans Leeds, supporting transgender, non-binary and gender expansive people who may face stigma and barriers when accessing healthcare
-	UserZoom, used to recruit a broader sample for unmoderated testing

## Unmoderated testing

Participants: 32
Participants were shown one of the following result types:

-	HPV positive (6 participants)
-	HPV negative (5 participants)
-	Unavailable result (5 participants)
-	Rejected result (5 participants)
-	HPV negative aged 60+ (11 participants)

## Moderated testing

Moderated testing
Participants: 10
We ensured a mix of ages and screening attendance behaviours.
Focus areas:

-	Tone of voice and emotional response
-	Whether content meets user needs
-	Understanding of results and next steps

## What we learned

### Results were generally well understood

Participants found the content clear and were able to identify next steps.

However, some users wanted more information to feel fully informed, particularly around symptoms and what results mean for their health.

### HPV negative results needed more clarity

Some participants were unsure what a negative result meant long term.

We improved this by:

-	Clarifying that no high-risk HPV was found
-	Strengthening the link between HPV, abnormal cells and cervical cancer

### Unavailable results caused the most concern

Unavailable results were the least well understood and often seen as inconvenient.

Participants questioned:

-	why a result could not be obtained
-	what this meant for their health

We added clearer explanations and reassurance, including next steps after a second unavailable result.

### Sexual health clinics were not seen as relevant

References to sexual health clinics were unclear to most participants.

-	Only 1 participant saw this as a viable option
-	Most preferred their GP due to familiarity and convenience

This suggests the association between cervical screening and sexual health clinics is weak.

## What we did next

### Working with clinical safety and communications

Following the research, we held a collaborative session with clinical safety officers and the communications team to review findings and agree content changes.

We used a Mural board with each results screen laid out. As a group, we worked through each result message and added suggested updates using digital sticky notes.

This allowed us to:

-	review research findings alongside the content
-	discuss clinical accuracy and tone of voice in real time
-	agree changes collaboratively across disciplines

All updates were agreed during the session and then sent for final sign-off.

This approach reduced the need for multiple rounds of feedback over email and made the sign-off process more efficient.
