In our recent research into breast screening on mobile vans we investigated current ways of working, connectivity challenges, and the role of fallbacks in maintaining a resilient service. 


## What we learned 

Our research showed that a fallback process will always be needed when delivering screening on mobile vans. However, we do not yet know what the best replacement for existing offline processes might be. Instead, this work has helped identify key areas for exploration. 


### A fallback process remains essential 

Mobile screening vans need a way to continue operating when connectivity is unavailable or systems are disrupted. NBSS Daybook, the offline version of NBSS (National Breast Screening System) currently fulfils this role. There may be alternative approaches that better support working offline with the new web-based digital service, Run Breast Screening in England (Rubie). 

### Connectivity varies significantly 

The experiences of Breast Screening Offices (BSOs) differ considerably. Some services work effectively online, while others experience frequent connectivity issues and have concerns about whether a web-based service can meet their needs. This means any future solution must work across a wide range of operating environments. 

Connectivity is influenced by many factors, including network coverage, internet providers, trust infrastructure, hardware on vans, and image transfer technologies. There are different ways we will seek to address some of these. Some will be addressed through the design and implementation of Rubie. Some will be addressed through influencing policy change and working at a national and regional level to affect change. 


## Designing a flexible, resilient service that users can trust  

Rather than thinking about designing for mobile sites and static sites separately, we should be working towards creating flexible services that can operate in a variety of environments and can remain resilient under constraints.   

We think there is value in running small-scale experiments with Rubie on mobile vans as early as possible. Working with BSOs that have [different connectivity profiles](https://design-history.prevention-services.nhs.uk/breast-screening-pathway/2026/06/connectivity-fallbacks-mobile-vans/) will help us understand how the service performs under varying conditions, identify opportunities to improve performance, and explore possible fallback processes. 

 

### Testing Rubie on mobile vans 

We would like to explore: 

- how Rubie performs on cellular internet connections (3G, 4G, 5G), to highlight parts of the interface to improve so that they work efficiently with limited connectivity   

- how Rubie might interact with other technology on vans and integrate with trust networks. For example, how the gateway could facilitate the transfer of imaging data. The gateway is planned to be installed in each trust and act as a link between Rubie and trust systems 

- where slower connection speeds could cause bottlenecks in the service. For example, when transferring images across systems 

 

### Devices to support working in different environments   

We would like to explore the use of different devices used during screening appointments, including laptops and tablets. This should consider usability, ergonomics, cost, and support requirements.  

 

### Fallback processes 

Finally, we want to better understand what a future fallback process could look like. This could include testing both digital offline approaches and paper-based processes to ensure services can continue operating safely and effectively when connectivity is intermittent or unavailable. We will run a series of short experiments to assess the operational risks and benefits of each solution. 

We would like to explore: 

- prototyping and testing paper-based fallback processes  

- the time it might take to switch between fallbacks and the online service, to minimise interruptions to running clinics 

- offline access to participant information; information being captured offline and synchronised later; storing data in advance of clinics 

- user authentication when offline 

 

## Using national policy levers to raise standards of connectivity across England 

There are things we can do nationally to help the breast screening services to be operationally ready for using Rubie. 

 

### Standards and connectivity provision 

The research highlighted a lack of visibility around connectivity services available to BSOs. Future work could explore creating clearer national guidance, sharing information about supplier performance, and helping services choose connectivity options that best meet local needs. 

If we can better understand connectivity technologies used across the country, then we can share this knowledge with BSOs so that they can learn from each other's working practices. 

 

### Develop connectivity profiles that articulate the variation in practice around the country  

Some places might need more help than others to get their vans working online in a stable and reliable way.  

We plan to group the BSOs based on what we know about their current set-up and identify ways we can help each group. This could be identifying funding options and communicating with trusts and regional commissioners.   

 

### Service specification and network access 

Questions remain about whether minimum connectivity standards could improve equity of service provision and what impact this might have on costs or feasibility in areas that struggle with connectivity. We will make changes to the service specification that state what minimum connectivity we expect for mobile vans.  

We would also like to understand how mobile breast screening might benefit from network strengthening programmes, such as the [Shared Rural Network](https://srn.org.uk/forecast-coverage-improvements/) as it improves network coverage across the country. 

 

## Getting ahead  

There are lots of different ways that we can influence the improvement of connectivity for mobile screening vans, through technology, standards, and procurement.  

It is important that we look at these things now because some change will take a long time to implement. We're also looking at this now because what we hope to learn will shape how Rubie will operate, and scale. The later we leave the exploration, the more constrained our choices will be by decisions that have already been made.  

All the experimentation in this next phase of work will help us towards delivering a resilient digital service that works in all environments where breast screening takes place.
