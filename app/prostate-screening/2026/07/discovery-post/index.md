Everyone has BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes. They usually protect us from cancer. People with a BRCA2 gene variant have a significantly higher risk of certain cancers including prostate and breast cancer. Many of them already know this. They have been through genetic testing. They have spoken to clinicians about lifestyle and their future. They have likely had difficult conversations with their family members.

## What we were asked to explore

Can we build a digital first screening pathway that reaches biological men with the BRCA2 gene, and aged between 45 and 61?

Can we do it using existing pathway components?

We had 6 weeks to find out, aiming to have something ready for participants by early 2027.

## The tight constraints we worked within

Discovery is rarely straightforward and we had a few barriers to overcome for ours. The service doesn't exist yet, so there are no staff to speak to: no live data to learn from. The timing of government sign-off for prostate screening also meant we couldn't discuss our work openly. Most significantly, it also meant we couldn't speak directly to people with BRCA2.

These constraints limited our understanding of our participant group:

1. What do they know?
2. What don’t they know?
3. What have they experienced so far regarding prostate screening
4. How might they feel when we first reach out to them?

However, we weren't starting with nothing. We had findings from the pre-discovery as a foundation to build on.

## How we overcame these constraints

### By trying to understand who we are designing for

Without the ability to speak directly to our cohort, we spent a significant amount of time on desktop research. This helped us piece together who these people are, and what their journey tends to look like before they would ever encounter our service.

We discovered that people with the BRCA2 gene have likely already been through a lot in terms of testing and managing the outcomes of the results. They have probably been through:

- genetic testing
- results conversations with medical professionals
- decisions about their own health and their family's health

By the time someone with BRCA2 gets invited for a PSA test, they may well have experience of navigating the health system. That does not mean the experience will feel familiar or welcome.

This context shaped how we are likely to think about the pathway and how we communicate with our cohort from the start.

### By learning from other teams

Although we couldn’t speak to participants and didn’t have our own staff to work with, we could speak to other services: teams who had been doing this work longer than us and already have established pathways. We spoke to services across screening including CaaS, SPI, and the Vaccinations appointment booking team. We asked them:

- what their pathways look like
- what works
- what they would do differently if they could

Understanding the lessons learned by other teams will help us avoid similar mistakes. It will also help identify what might work for a prostate screening service so we don’t try to reinvent the wheel. 3 things stood out.

1. Timed appointments can be more successful than open invites. Services that invite participants to a specific appointment slot generally see better uptake than those that ask people to contact and book themselves. For a population who may already feel anxious or uncertain about what screening means for them, reducing that friction matters. We want to test this properly in our alpha.
2. GP dependency creates unnecessary barriers. Registering people through their GP can introduce delays and drop-off — particularly for a cohort who may already be navigating a lot. GPs should absolutely receive results, but they shouldn't sit in the critical path for invitation and booking.
3. A phone-based support model makes sense to start with. The HPV self-testing service has built something genuinely good in its telephone support offer. For our small initial cohort, we think that a one to one call will be the best place to start. This will make it possible to:
   - discuss the PSA test and medication
   - book an appointment
   - discuss any concerns
   - learn more about our participants and behaviours than we would attempting a digital method first

## What we found

It is feasible to build a pathway targeting this specific population: one grounded in what already works elsewhere. Instead of launching something huge, our approach will be to start small and develop based on first hand findings and data that we gather as we go along.

We expect the participant journey to move through these steps:

1.                             Participant is identified as eligible.
2.                             Participant receives a pre- notification message.
3.                             Participant is contacted by phone to discuss medication and preparation.
4.                             Participant’s PSA test appointment is booked.
5.                             Participant attends their appointment.
6.                             Participant’s sample is processed at a lab.
7.                             Participant receives their results.

There's still a lot to learn here about our participants as well as our internal users like call handlers, CDC staff and lab staff. We will use our alpha to answer our questions and fill our knowledge gaps.

## What's next

We start alpha in July, with site visits to CDCs being planned already. We're aiming for private beta in early 2027.

There is real momentum behind this work. Prostate cancer affects a lot of men in the UK. It has been a very recent political talking point, and featured in the press and media, with public figures like Jeremy Clarkson speaking openly about his own experiences with prostate cancer.

We want to design and build a pathway that considers our participants experiences and offers a simple journey through prostate cancer screening.
