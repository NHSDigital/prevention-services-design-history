When we formed as a team in early 2026, we decided to focus on clinic setup and appointment booking. One of our first steps was reviewing existing data and insights to identify gaps and decide what to explore next.  

We identified a need to learn more about how Breast Screening Office (BSO) staff set up clinics and schedule appointments and any challenges they face.  

This design history covers the three most important findings from our research with BSOs:
1. setting up clinics is a manual process
2. special appointments have to be managed manually
3. staff spend a lot of time managing rebookings

## What did we do?

Between March and April 2026, we visited 5 BSOs and interviewed 1 remotely to learn more about their approaches to clinic creation, management and appointment booking. 

We selected BSOs in a range of locations and serving varied populations so that we could understand the different challenges they face and how this impacts the way they manage clinics and appointments. 

Where possible, we visited BSOs in person so that we could observe current processes and better understand the challenges admin staff face. 

## What did we learn?
### Setting up clinics is a manual process
We heard from BSOs that there are several manual steps involved in getting clinics set up before staff can begin booking appointments.  

First, if the clinic is due to take place in a new location, admin staff need to create a new clinic code for the location in the National Breast Screening System (NBSS). If it’s a location they’ve been to before, they can reuse the clinic code from previous visits.  

Then, BSOs need to set the schedule for the clinic, specifying dates, opening hours and appointment slots. These settings are repeated across each week the clinic will run.  

Once the broad schedule has been set and the clinic opened, this capacity becomes available in NBSS's calendar view, which allows BSOs to see available capacity for each day within the clinic at a glance. Any changes to individual days or slots then need to be made manually.

We heard from BSOs that they sometimes need to hold particular slots for things like rebookings, or to block out time for participants needing a double appointment slot. To do this, admin staff must block individual appointment slots for each day manually in NBSS, known as 'stopping' slots. This is time-consuming and repetitive because there’s no way to apply this across multiple days if BSOs want to block slots in a regular pattern. 

Reducing the amount of repetitive, manual work involved in getting clinics set up in future would save BSOs a considerable amount of time and effort.

### Special appointments have to be managed manually
Some participants may need a special appointment – for example, participants with access needs may need screening at an accessible site, and participants with breast implants may need a longer appointment and to be screened by specific mammographers who are trained in the [Eklund technique](/manage-breast-screening/2026/03/recording-images-for-participants-with-breast-implants/).  

There’s no automated way to get data about which participants need special appointments into NBSS. Instead, BSOs are reliant on GP practices responding to requests for this information in advance of screening, or participants notifying the BSO when they receive their invite. If the BSO doesn’t get this information, participants may be invited to an unsuitable appointment which could lead to them attending and not being able to be screened. 

Once BSOs are made aware that someone needs a special appointment, they can record this in NBSS, selecting from a list of reasons to indicate why the participant needs a special appointment. This information can then be used to help the BSO book them into a suitable appointment.  

Matching participants with special requirements to an appropriate appointment type and location is highly manual and time-consuming, and relies heavily on BSOs’ local knowledge and interpretation of participants’ needs. 

Managing special appointments is one of the parts of the clinics and appointments process that takes the longest so a more efficient way to do this in future would be a big help for admin staff. 

### Staff spend a lot of time managing rebookings
BSOs invite participants to timed appointments, rather than asking participants to book their own appointment, as this generally leads to better attendance. However, we heard from BSOs that they get a lot of incoming contact from participants asking to change their appointment, which can take a lot of time to manage.  

While there isn’t currently a national service to support participants to reschedule their appointments online, two of the BSOs we visited had built their own web rebooking solutions. These offer participants a convenient web interface for asking to change their appointment, while helping reduce some of the time staff spend handling phone calls. However, staff still have to process these requests manually, with details being received by email and admin staff then needing to go into NBSS and manually change the participant’s appointment.   

A more automated system for allowing participants to reschedule their appointments online in future would make a big impact for BSOs, helping to reduce some of the time and effort admin staff currently spend on managing rebookings.

As only a few BSOs currently offer the option for participants to rebook their appointment online, introducing a national service for online rebookings would help ensure a more consistent participant experience in England, giving everyone the option to rebook their appointment online if they wish to.

## What's next?
Visiting and speaking with BSOs has given us a clearer understanding of current processes involved in scheduling breast screening appointments and the challenges staff face.  

We’re using what we learned to develop early designs for how clinics and appointments could be managed in a future service. 

Our next step will be to take designs to BSOs to get feedback, which we will use to shape future improvements.
