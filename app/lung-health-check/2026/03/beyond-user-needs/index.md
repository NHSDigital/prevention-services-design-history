We are designing a new digital service for the [lung cancer screening](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/lung-cancer-screening/) programme. The purpose of the service is to work out whether someone should be offered a CT scan to check their lungs. This service already exists as a questionnaire delivered over the phone, but we are exploring whether a digital offering would be desirable to users and clinically safe. We wrote about this in more detail in a [previous design history](https://design-history.prevention-services.nhs.uk/lung-health-check/2025/08/lung-health-check-prototype/).

## Testing our new designs 

We recently completed a round of user research in which we focused on testing the prototype of our service with people who have complex smoking histories. This included people who had used a variety of different tobacco products in their lives and those who had stopped and restarted smoking at various points.  

We wanted to test with these users, because we had made a lot of changes to our designs, based on what we learned in previous rounds of research. In another design history, we wrote about [how we tried to find the right balance](https://design-history.prevention-services.nhs.uk/lung-health-check/2026/02/impact-smoking-questions/)in these designs, between capturing enough information for the risk calculation to be accurate, and keeping users engaged so they don't drop out of our service.  

The research sessions were remote, taking place on video calls, and involved a researcher guiding participants through the prototype, so they were only shown the questions which would be relevant to them.

## Moving beyond user needs 

Since work began on this project in June 2025, we have conducted 6 rounds of remote user interviews. We have also carried out two rounds of face-to-face research and observed some of the phone calls which take place in the existing service.  

Early in our research, we began documenting the user needs which were identified. We ensured that every user need came directly from an observed finding (what happened) and insight (why it happened), based on what we observed in our interviews. This was extremely useful in helping us design our service based on solving real problems for real people. For example: ‘As a user of the service, I need sensitive questions to be clear and non-judgemental… so that I feel comfortable sharing honest answers.’  

As we have spoken to more potential users, we have learned more about the way they are likely to experience our service. We have surfaced lots of new findings and insights, but these don’t always correspond to a new user need. Even when they do, the new user needs are often very similar or only subtly different to some of our existing ones.  

Compressing the observations into an existing user need also risks losing the richness of the findings and insights, which are valuable sources of information in of themselves. At this point, identifying new user needs has become less useful as an approach to analysing the things we learn.  

On top of this, some of the most important insights that emerged were about how people felt when they answered questions about smoking. How they expected the service to work, and their previous experiences with health. These parts of the way people experience a service are difficult to translate neatly into the format of ‘as a user, I need X, so that Y’, without losing important nuance.  

We know that lung cancer screening is an emotive subject for many people, and that talking about smoking history can evoke strong feelings throughout the journey. This means that emotions, mental models and contexts are especially important for our service.  

## Analysing complex user experiences

To make sense of our findings and insights without losing any of the important detail, nuance and richness, we needed an analysis approach that helped us explain what we were seeing. The framework we used focuses on 7 areas: users’ goals, behaviours, emotions, mental models, pain points, contexts and tasks. This approach was inspired by [an article and talk given at UX Brighton 2018, by Will Myddleton](https://www.myddelton.co.uk/blog/research-heresies/). 

One major benefit of this framework was that it allowed us to look at the different dimensions of user experiences in their own right. Structuring our findings and insights across these 7 lenses meant that we could keep the richness of the observations, while focusing them in a way that makes sense for a multidisciplinary audience. For example, where designers might focus on the tasks, mental models and emotions, our product and delivery colleagues can see the goals, behaviours and constraints we need to consider.  

Sometimes user needs unintentionally frame users as ‘request makers’ who are making demands of the service. The ‘7 lenses’ framework shifts the conversation away from what users are asking the service to do, and towards how they are actually experiencing it. Below are some of the main things we learned about our users’ experiences in this round of research.  

## Goals: what users are trying to achieve 

The users we spoke to were primarily aiming to get an early insight into their lung health. Taking preventative action was a key goal for users, but they wanted to feel like their efforts were actually worthwhile. And that the process wouldn’t be too burdensome or anxiety-inducing.  

Another key goal was getting a clear answer to the question: ‘do I need a scan or not?’ Users aimed to leave our service with a transparent and decisive outcome, with an obvious next step if a follow-up appointment or scan was needed.  

## Behaviours: how users interact with health services 

We saw that most of the people we spoke to were already active users of digital health tools, particularly the NHS app. These users were comfortable accessing health information online and often used digital tools to view results before receiving letters. This meant that they were comfortable with the idea of taking part in an online service for lung screening and that they would expect to see results quickly.  

It also meant that the idea of using NHS login to authenticate themselves before answering questions about their health was familiar, acceptable and sometimes even welcome. Many were used to logging into the NHS app via Face ID or similar authentication methods, so they expected that NHS login would work in the same way. Several people told us that they would struggle to remember their NHS account password, because they are used to logging in with biometrics.  

People often told us that they preferred the idea of a digital service to a phone call, when it comes to personal topics like smoking. They were also more comfortable giving rough, approximate answers when explicitly told that estimates were okay. 

## Emotions: what users feel throughout the journey 

Screening can be a very emotional experience from the moment somebody receives an invitation to take part. In our research we heard that seeing the words ‘lung cancer’ on the start page of our prototype caused spikes of fear. We also heard that questions about smoking often triggered discomfort or shame. Asking someone to recall their past smoking habits prompted people to reflect on the way that past decisions might have impacted their risk of getting lung cancer.   

We know that our service needs to be sensitive to these emotions, which is something we have thought about a lot in our design process. We saw that some of our new designs for smoking history questions were able to ease the fear, guilt or shame people felt, by making the service feel supportive, neutral and clear.  

## Mental models: how users think the system works 

How people expect the service to work is extremely important, because we know that if you fail to meet people’s expectations you can lose their trust. We need to be aware of any areas where our service is not doing what they expect it to do so we can work out what we can do differently. 

We saw that users expect our service to give them a result. This isn’t a problem for future versions of our service, because eventually we will be telling people whether they need to come in for a face-to-face appointment and a scan. In the pilot version of our service though, which is being introduced early in 2026, we will not be able to do that. Instead, users will have to get their result over the phone.  

## Pain points: where things break down for users 

Users were confused when they learned that they would still need to complete the phone version of the service. Some found this very surprising and frustrating. Others understood that the digital version was just a trial and that we would be offering a small financial incentive if they took part.   

Editing answers was also problematic. For most people, being shown a ‘check your answers page’ was the first time they had ever seen their full smoking history written down. For some, this was quite confronting and not something they had expected or wanted to see. Other users worried that if they did decide to change one of their answers, they would have to repeat long sections of the journey.   

## Contexts: the situations shaping users’ experiences 

We learned that lots of our participants were navigating stressful and sometimes complex personal health backgrounds. This included chronic conditions, recent diagnoses, or experiences of serious illness in themselves or others close to them. This is important for us to understand, because it shapes how people are likely to feel when they come into contact with our service.  

## Tasks: what users are actually trying to do in the journey 

On a practical level, users were trying to understand why they had been invited and whether it was routine or meant that there was something wrong. They wanted to log in quickly, answer questions honestly, and feel confident about what information was relevant and how precise it needed to be. 

Users also told us that they valued being able to review and change their answers easily. They said that they were used to doing this in other areas of their lives, like dealing with tax on HMRC online services. Above all, they felt they were trying to get a clear indication of whether they needed a scan and what would happen next. 

## How this research will shape our next steps 

Looking across these 7 areas helped us build a more complete picture of how people with complex smoking histories experience our service. It allowed us to understand what people were trying to do, how they felt, what they expected, and their contexts that shaped how they experience it. As we continue to work on our designs, this has given us a stronger foundation for making decisions that are sensitive and grounded in real experiences.
