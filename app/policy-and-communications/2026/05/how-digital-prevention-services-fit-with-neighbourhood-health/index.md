We ran a discovery across the Digital Prevention Services Portfolio (DPSP) to understand how our products work with the NHS’s new [neighbourhood health](https://www.kingsfund.org.uk/insight-and-analysis/long-reads/what-is-neighbourhood-health) model.

Neighbourhood health is central to the [10‑year plan](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/10-year-health-plan-for-england-fit-for-the-future), with digital positioned as an enabler. Neighbourhood health services will become the main way people access care, organised around defined populations and local priorities, integrating funding and delivery at place, and tailoring interventions to specific cohorts.

But DPSP products are largely built as national programmes – designed centrally, with national governance, mandates and standardised deployment pathways. Without adapting, deployments risk becoming fragmented, requiring repeated and high‑cost local engagement, and widening the gap between the national digital offer and neighbourhood services.

## What we did

The Innovation and Analysis team undertook an internal discovery to understand how existing DPSP products and services interact with neighbourhood health models and what this looks like in practice. This included engagement with teams across [Cohorting as a Service (CaaS)](https://digital.nhs.uk/services/cohorting-as-a-service-caas), [Select People for Invitation (SPI)](https://digital.nhs.uk/services/select-people-for-invitation-spi), [Managing My Health](/managing-my-health/), [breast screening](https://digital.nhs.uk/services/screening-services/breast-screening-services) and home testing. We wanted to understand:

- how products are currently deployed locally
- what local partners experience when engaging with DPSP
- how partner selection, governance and learning are managed across teams
- where there are opportunities for a more coordinated neighbourhood approach

## What we learnt

### Some things need to flex locally, others must stay national

Teams agreed that capabilities like targeting, messaging and content tailoring need to flex to meet local needs. But eligibility, clinical assurance, cohort definition and risk management must stay nationally defined. Clear guardrails are needed – enough flexibility to support adoption, enough consistency to keep delivery clinically and technically safe.

### Governance is both a barrier and an opportunity

Current information governance and legal frameworks are built to assure national programmes. As local systems develop neighbourhood health services, governance will need to evolve to enable local working without relying on bespoke assurance for every deployment.

### Teams share data sources, but not a shared approach to data

Products rely on a common set of national datasets – hospital episode statistics and similar cohorting methodologies – but there's no shared approach to defining, governing or reusing data for neighbourhood delivery. Greater alignment would support consistent learning and comparability across places.

### Partner selection is organic, not strategic

DPSP tracks partner engagement through a central tracker and open weekly notes, but local engagement and pilot selection have developed organically – shaped by relationships and opportunity rather than consistent selection criteria. Learning from deployments isn't always shared across DPSP. A more structured approach would strengthen the evidence base for scaling.

### Teams want more cross‑DPSP visibility

Teams already work in the open through portfolio show‑and‑tells and All Hands calls. But there's appetite for more cross‑DPSP visibility, particularly to coordinate neighbourhood health work with partners.

## Recommendations

The discovery pointed to three areas where DPSP needs to adapt:

1. On the DPSP offer – clarify what can be flexed locally and what stays nationally defined, and establish a consistent governance approach for using national products in neighbourhood settings.

2. On partners – take a more strategic and coordinated approach to partner selection and engagement, and align internally to share learning and identify opportunities to test and scale a neighbourhood offer.

3. On data – develop a clearer, more consistent approach to how national and local data support neighbourhood delivery, and improve alignment across products to enable shared learning between neighbourhoods.
