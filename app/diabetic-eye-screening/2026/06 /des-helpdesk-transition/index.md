The programme successfully reached a significant milestone last week with the transfer of the DES Helpdesk function from Health Information Consulting (HIC), a third-party supplier, to the National Service Desk (NSD).

The DES Helpdesk provides specialist support for organisations and users involved in the delivery of national screening and cohorting services. The team manages a range of enquiries, including user access requests, operational queries, incident resolution, process guidance, and support for service users across the programme. Maintaining continuity of this support function is essential to ensuring users can access timely assistance and guidance when required.

Since the service was established, the DES Helpdesk has been delivered by HIC as part of the wider supplier-led operating model. While this model successfully supported the initial delivery of the service, it also resulted in specialist operational knowledge being concentrated within supplier teams and created a degree of dependency on third-party support. 

As the programme continues to mature and NHS teams build capability and expertise internally, responsibility for key support services is progressively transferring into NHS operations. This approach is designed to strengthen organisational resilience, reduce dependency on third-party suppliers, improve knowledge retention within NHS teams, and support the long-term sustainability of national services.

Before proceeding with the transition, the programme considered a range of options for the future delivery of the DES Helpdesk function.

These included continuing with the existing supplier-led model, introducing additional supplier support to increase resilience, and transferring responsibility into an NHS-managed service. Each option was assessed against key criteria including service continuity, organisational resilience, knowledge retention, long-term sustainability, and the ability to support future service changes.

While the supplier-led model had successfully supported the service since its introduction, the review identified opportunities to strengthen resilience by embedding specialist knowledge and operational expertise within NHS teams. The National Service Desk was already providing nationally managed support services through established service management processes and governance arrangements, making it well placed to take on the DES Helpdesk function.

The transfer to NSD was therefore selected as the preferred option because it offered the best balance of service continuity, operational resilience, knowledge retention, and long-term sustainability, while also supporting the programme's wider objective of bringing key operational capabilities into NHS-managed services.

### Building capability and resilience 

A key objective of the transition has been to develop knowledge and capability within NHS teams while maintaining service continuity for users.

The National Service Desk was identified as a natural home for the DES Helpdesk function due to its established service management processes, experienced operational teams, and expertise in delivering nationally managed support services. Integrating DES Helpdesk activities into NSD enables support to be delivered through an existing and resilient service model while strengthening internal ownership of specialist knowledge.

Through onboarding the DES Helpdesk function, National Service Desk colleagues have developed expertise across new products, services, and operational processes. This has increased organisational capability and resilience while reducing reliance on external suppliers for day-to-day service delivery.

The transition has also created additional capacity within supplier organisations, including HIC, enabling them to focus on ongoing cohorting activities and preparations for future service transfers.


### Maintaining a seamless service 

The transition was carefully planned to ensure minimal disruption to users. Existing support channels have been maintained, allowing organisations and service users to continue accessing assistance through familiar routes.

While the change represents a transfer of operational responsibility, users continue to receive support through established processes. The move into NSD also provides opportunities for greater consistency in ticket management, triage, escalation, reporting, and service oversight as support activities become integrated into established national service management processes.


## Key outcomes delivered: 

The transition has enabled:

-	Successful migration of the DES Helpdesk function to the National Service Desk
-	Development of internal expertise across new products and service areas
-	Increased capability within NHS-managed support services
-	Reduced dependency on third-party suppliers for service delivery
-	Greater supplier capacity to support wider programme activity
-	Improved organisational resilience through the transfer of specialist knowledge
-	A strong foundation for future service transfers


### Lessons learned 

As the first service transition of its kind within the programme, the migration has provided valuable insights that will inform future transfers.

Key lessons identified include:

-	Early engagement between supplier and NHS teams is critical to effective knowledge transfer and operational readiness
-	Structured shadowing and joint-working activities help build confidence and capability before service handover
-	Clearly documented processes and escalation routes support service continuity throughout transition periods
-	Regular communication with stakeholders helps manage expectations and maintain confidence in delivery
-	A phased approach enables risks and dependencies to be identified and addressed before go-live

These lessons will be incorporated into future transition planning to support successful service transfers across the programme.


## Supporting future change

The successful delivery of this first transition demonstrates the value of the phased approach being adopted across the programme.

Over the next year, additional service areas and query types will progressively move into the National Service Desk, further expanding its role as a central support function. This evolution will continue alongside the rollout of the new cohorting solution, helping to create a more integrated, sustainable, and resilient support model for national services.

As cohorting work progresses, the programme will continue to build on the experience gained through the DES Helpdesk transition, bringing further supplier-managed services into NHS operations where appropriate. This will strengthen long-term sustainability, enhance organisational capability, and support the delivery of national service objectives.
